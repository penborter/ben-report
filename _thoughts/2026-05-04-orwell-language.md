From [*Politics and the English Language*](https://www.orwellfoundation.com/the-orwell-foundation/orwell/essays-and-other-works/politics-and-the-english-language/) by George Orwell. Most of the 'issues' he raises I think are more common today than when he was writing (1946). The euphemism, the vagueness, the removal of substance — the ease of slipping into this mode and never coming out again.

On the quote below, Lydia Davis talked about a similar thing in [*Essays One*](/books#Essays_One) about the use of old English words instead of those with some other foreign (even if well-embedded) root.

The jargon peculiar to Marxist writing (*hyena*, *hangman*, *cannibal*, *petty bourgeois*, *these gentry*, *lackey*, *flunkey*, *mad dog*, *White Guard*, etc.) consists largely of words translated from Russian, German, or French; but the normal way of coining a new word is to use a Latin or Greek root with the appropriate affix and, where necessary, the *-ize* formation. It is often easier to make up words of this kind (*deregionalize*, *impermissible*, *extramarital*, *non-fragmentatory* and so forth) than to think up the English words that will cover one's meaning. The result, in general, is an increase in slovenliness and vagueness."
